An integrated optical circuit is one or more circuits composed of solid-state optical components on a semiconductor or dielectric substrate. Components include light sources, optical filters, photodetectors, and thin-film optical waveguides. An example of an integrated optical circuit is an opto-isolator (or opto-coupler) which allows a circuit to interact with another one while remaining electrically buffered from it.

All optical devices can be considered as the optical equivalent of the transistor based elementary devices in electronic circuitry, for example logic gates, amplifiers etc. Like in these devices where the current is controlled or switched by another current, in optical integrated devices the control or the switching of a light beam is done by another light beam. Integrated optics is basically several different optical components into one package resulting in an integrated optical circuit, in a manner analogous to electrical integrated circuits.

At the elementary level there is a fundamental difference between electronic integrated circuits and an optical integrated circuit. Electronic integrated circuits rely on Electrons that show strong interaction and obey Fermi Statistics, whereas Optical integration relies on Photons that obey Bose-Einstein Statistics and do not interact directly. In integrated optics the interaction between beams is achieved via a suitable Non Linear medium.

This technology is of interest to computing due to:
* Optical transmission speed is 21cm/nanosecond faster than copper.
* A lack of EMI between optical signals, eliminating crosstalk and stray capacitances in computers.
